Terms List · HDSR AI Leadership Intensive

Working glossary for the course. Terms grouped by where they first matter. Definitions paraphrase the cited sources; quotes are marked.

HDSR Core Concepts (know these — they’re the faculty’s vocabulary)

Vico Effect — Coined by David Leslie (Alan Turing Institute) in the closing editorial of HDSR’s Future Shock special issue. Named for philosopher Giambattista Vico’s insight that all history — including the history of technology — is a product of human making.

The Vico Effect is “the enduring interplay between anthropogenic innovation, entrenched sociohistorical dynamics, and humanity’s irrepressible critical and self-reflective capacities.” — David Leslie, HDSR

In practice it’s the antidote to technological determinism: because AI is a human creation, its direction can be changed, course-corrected, and collectively transformed — it is not an autonomous force carving an inevitable path. Leslie’s evidence: DeepSeek’s 2025 release (“ingenuity bites back”) upended assumptions that AI progress belonged only to hyperscalers. Leslie also names a fourth, reflexive vector — the one most useful for governance work: “Because AI technology is a human and thus historical creation, we must understand and investigate it as such. We must interrogate it reflexively as arising from our all-too-human motivations, interests, norms, and institutions. We must thereby come to know AI as a product of our making and, for this reason, as a product whose direction we can change, course correct, and collectively transform in accordance with a shared vision of the future.” For your playbook: this is the philosophical grounding for why governance and human oversight are legitimate — AI outcomes are choices, not destiny — and the quote works verbatim as an epigraph for your Module 3 governance section. Source: https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/bcp7n3bs

Technological determinism — The (mistaken) assumption that technology develops on an inevitable path, causing history rather than being shaped by it. Leslie & Meng warn it “leads us into complacency in the face of potential social harm.” The framing to challenge whenever someone says AI adoption is “inevitable.”

Future Shock — From Toffler, adopted by HDSR Special Issue 5: the disorientation produced by too much technological change too fast. The special issue’s framing question is how to grapple with GenAI’s shock without paralysis.

Wonder / Worry / Winnow — Xiao-Li Meng’s triad from his Winter 2026 editorial Navigating the AI Safari: wonder boldly (explore new value), worry responsibly (take risks seriously without paralysis), winnow relentlessly (filter hype from substance, prioritize ruthlessly). Maps directly to your Module 1 work: vision (wonder), risk appetite (worry), use-case prioritization (winnow). Source: https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/m4mz70zp/release/5

Mindware — Meng’s term for the learnable traits that enable sound human reasoning and decision-making with data and AI — the human counterpart to hardware and software. Upgrading mindware, not just deploying tools, is the point of executive AI education.

Module 1 · Strategy & Readiness

AI readiness assessment — Structured diagnostic across pillars (data, talent, governance, operating model, infrastructure) measuring preparedness to deploy and scale AI. The gaps are strategy input, not embarrassments.

Use-case prioritization matrix — Scoring grid ranking AI opportunities on weighted criteria — typically value (35–45%), feasibility (25–35%), time-to-value, reuse, risk. Course version: feasibility × business value × organizational fit.

Problem tractability — C3.ai’s criterion: not whether AI could address a problem, but whether the problem is well-defined enough for current AI to solve reliably.

AI vision statement — One-paragraph articulation of how AI creates new value (capability, market, customer outcome) — not just efficiency. The course explicitly pushes past efficiency framing.

Risk appetite — Explicit statement of how much and what kinds of AI risk the org will accept — and what it won’t do. The exclusion list is what separates strategy from wish list.

Module 2 · Agentic AI

Agentic system — Umbrella term for LLM-powered systems that use tools and take actions. Anthropic’s key distinction: workflows orchestrate LLMs through predefined code paths; agents dynamically direct their own processes and tool use. Use the simplest thing that works.

Augmented LLM — The basic building block: an LLM enhanced with retrieval, tools, and memory.

Orchestration patterns — The five canonical workflow patterns: prompt chaining (sequential steps with gates), routing (classify then dispatch), parallelization (sectioning or voting), orchestrator-workers (central LLM delegates dynamically), evaluator-optimizer (generate/critique loop).

MCP (Model Context Protocol) — Open standard for connecting AI agents to external tools and data; implement once, integrate an ecosystem. The USB-C of agent integrations.

Human-in-the-loop (HITL) — Checkpoint where a human approves, corrects, or overrides agent action. In your prototype these are the “governance hooks” that make it executive-credible.

Guardrails — Technical controls constraining agent behavior: input/output screening, refusal behavior, scope limits, rate limits, approval gates.

Context engineering — The successor to prompt engineering: instead of just writing good instructions, curating the entire set of tokens the model sees at each step (system prompt, tools, examples, retrieved data, message history). Anthropic’s guiding principle: “find the smallest set of high-signal tokens that maximize the likelihood of your desired outcome.” Key sub-concepts: context rot (recall accuracy degrades as context grows — attention is a finite budget, so more context ≠ better); just-in-time retrieval (agents keep lightweight references like file paths and load data on demand, instead of pre-loading everything); and three techniques for tasks longer than one context window — compaction (summarize and restart), structured note-taking (agent persists notes/memory outside the window and re-reads them), and sub-agent architectures (workers explore in isolated contexts, return distilled summaries to a lead agent). Course relevance: these are the design levers for your Month-2 prototype and the vocabulary for explaining why an agentic workflow does or doesn’t stay coherent. Source: https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/effective-context-engineering-for-ai-agents

Low-code / no-code — Platforms (Copilot Studio, n8n, etc.) that let non-engineers build AI workflows visually. Key selection tradeoff: workflow-controlled (you decide when the LLM runs) vs. agent-controlled (the model decides).

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — Grounding LLM output in retrieved documents to cut hallucination and use current/private knowledge.

Hallucination — Confident, fluent, wrong output. Mitigated (not eliminated) by RAG, grounding, evaluator loops, and HITL review.

Module 3 · Governance & Implementation

NIST AI RMF — US voluntary risk framework with four functions: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage. The structural backbone for most enterprise AI governance programs.

NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile) — Companion to the RMF identifying 12 risk categories unique to or amplified by GenAI, each mapped to governance actions. Updated April 2026.

EU AI Act — Binding EU regulation with risk tiers (prohibited / high-risk / limited / minimal). Most rules in force Aug 2, 2026; the June 2026 Digital Omnibus deferred Annex III high-risk obligations to Dec 2, 2027 (Annex I to Aug 2028). Transparency rules did take effect Aug 2026.

ISO/IEC 42001 — Certifiable AI management-system standard; the auditable counterpart to the voluntary NIST RMF.

AI system inventory — Register of all AI systems in use with risk classification, owner, and review cadence. Usually the first governance artifact regulators and boards ask for.

Kill-switch criteria — Predefined conditions under which an AI system is suspended or retired. Naming them in advance is what accountability looks like in practice.

Hard vs. soft ROI — Hard: direct P&L impact (revenue, conversion, cost per unit). Soft: productivity claims, NPS, sentiment. 2026 boards are fatigued by soft ROI; baseline cycle time, error rate, cost/unit, and throughput before deploying, and tie projections to workflow redesign.

Phased implementation — Pilot → controlled expansion → scale, with go/no-go gates tied to predefined metrics at each stage.

AI Transformation Playbook — Your capstone: consolidated strategy (M1) + prototype writeup (M2) + governance, metrics, and roadmap (M3) + executive summary.
